## Research significance

This page contains two independent eyewitness references to the **Skinner family residence on Railroad Street** (Mexico, NY), published in the same week Avery W. Skinner died (December 1937). It also contains the "Who's Who in America" biographical entry confirming Avery Warner Skinner as the son of **Timothy (V.) Skinner**, directly tying the family genealogy to the NRHP nomination subject.

## Full transcription

### Main article: "Avery W. Skinner" by R. Austin Backus

Just as the evening shadows were falling Monday afternoon, Mexico citizens were shocked to learn the news which quickly spread over the streets that one of our native sons, Avery W. Skinner, had suddenly passed away at Albany, in the Education building, where he had worked for many years.

Every Mexico citizen was proud of Avery Skinner. Proud of the fact that from our local educational institution he was able, by hard work and exceptional ability, to eventually became Director of Examinations and Inspections in the educational department of the State of New York. But the people of Mexico did not think of Doctor Skinner as the director of the finest educational system in the United States; rather they thought of him as a friend and neighbor.

He never for a moment lost sight of his high school Alma Mater and was quite fond to recall the past boastfully of Mexico Academy and high school.

When the Military Academy was discontinued in 1895 and the union free school district organized, the Board of Education engaged Doctor Skinner as the first principal of the union district. In a period of less than eight years after he had graduated from Mexico Academy, Doctor Skinner was named its principal. Allen T. Cook in a paper on "Mexico Academy in Recent Years," which he read at the time of the Centennial celebration in 1926, said, "Under Mr. Skinner, who was principal from 1895 to 1899, there was no period of doubtful experimentation but a quick integration of faculty, courses and departments. The work of the grades was unified and served as an introduction to four years of substantial academic work under sympathetic guidance."

His was the task, with the Board of Education, to close the three district schools then operating in the village, and organized a new faculty, new classrooms, and convince the people that this move was for the greater interests of the Mexico educational system.

The graduating classes in those days were, of course, much smaller than those of today, but the quality was of the best. One has only to know the abilities of the graduates of the years 1896 thru 1899, to fully appreciate the results which Doctor Skinner was able to obtain. That he inspired his boys and girls with a desire for more knowledge, causing them to continue into higher educational institutions, is shown in the fact that six of the twenty-one living alumni within the scope of his four years are very successful physicians and surgeons. Others are attorneys, prominent educational and successful business people. The list is as follows, as taken from alumni records:

**1896:** Dr. John Riley; Mrs. Walter Smith; D. Ward Howard; Mrs. Adelbert Borden; Orla A. Barton.

**1897:** None living.

**1898:** Dr. James W. Riley; Mabel V. Simons; Dr. Hugh Baker; Dr. Charles Curtis; George Davis; Frank Oxner; Dr. Earle A. Mowry; Ross L. Simons; Floyd Jones; Mrs. Mable Balch; Ford Markham; Mrs. Flora Wilson; Frank Jones.

**1899:** Mrs. Monica Biederman; Mrs. J. Edgar Unger; Dr. Charles Elkins.

For more than 30 years Doctor Skinner was in great demand as a speaker and lecturer before teachers, principals, and school superintendents.

At the time of Doctor Skinner's retirement in 1936 as director of the Examinations and Inspections Division, a position which he successfully held for 16 years, the New York State education magazine said of him: "In the administration of the Examinations and Inspections Division he has exerted a notable influence in improving instruction in the elementary and secondary schools of the state. His administration has been marked by important advances in the type of supervisory service afforded by his Division, and in the improvement in the form of Regents examinations prepared under his direction. Possessed of a sound judgment, Doctor Skinner has been an important figure in the teaching of school administrators and teachers. He was held in affectionate regard by his friends and acquaintances in Mexico and vicinity, and by school people, and people interested in schools throughout the state. As proof of the high esteem rendered him by his colleagues, Doctor Skinner was elected honorary president of the Associated Academic Principals, the organization of secondary school principals of the state in 1908. Only one other person, Dr. Charles F. Wheelock, former assistant commissioner for secondary education has been so honored."

[Article continues — second column:]

…staunch and firm in the belief that education, like all other worldly activities, must move forward. Thus from the days of the three village schools in the village of Mexico he had had an important part during two crucial and epoch-making transitional periods.

During the summer months and until election time this fall he made a daily inspection of construction progress on the new central school. He alone is responsible for Mexico having been selected as the first school in a village to have a department for physically handicapped. Many important conferences in which some of the leading educationalists of the state participated were held in the **study of his residence on Railroad Street**. Doctor Skinner worked with dynamic force in all activities and all undertakings of which he was a part. Long hours meant nothing to him; the future of the boys and girls of Mexico Oswego county and New York State did. He sacrificed for them until the very last and was at the time of his sudden death in conference with leading state educational officials to fully familiarize them to the emergency that now exists at our neighboring village of Pulaski. It is reported that he was in conference with Governor Lehman as late as last Friday.

One of the finest addresses I ever heard was delivered by Dr. Skinner at the time the Altmar high school was dedicated a few years ago. In this address he was able to trace Oswego county from its earliest pioneer days down thru the years, touching the high spots in its educational growth and interweaving his intimate knowledge of its politics, and its business activities and its farming from a close association with his father, grandfather, and uncle Dr. Charles R. Skinner.

It was in this address he referred to himself as in his sunset years. Calmly and without effort, he held an auditorium of interested listeners for nearly an hour.

The Board of Education in Mexico had planned to honor Doctor Skinner in every possible manner at the time of our new school dedication. He was to have been the center of the entire evening devoted to Mexico's educational history.

And so, as our new school becomes a going institutional center; as people enjoy the privileges of its library; listen to entertainments in its large and spacious auditorium; enjoy athletics and sports in its complete gymnasium; and as boys and girls grow in strength and knowledge; may each and every one never forget this man who was instrumental in making all these facilities in the building available to the people of this generation and to generations as yet unborn.

### DR. SKINNER — (Continued from front page)

[…administration at Oregon Agricultural College in 1927.]

One of the ablest educators in the history of the state, Doctor Skinner, in the administration of the Examinations and Inspections Division exerted a notable influence in improving instruction in the elementary and secondary schools of the state. Important progress in the type of supervisory service afforded by his Division and in the improvement in the form of Regents examinations prepared under his direction marked the highlights of his administration.

With a background rich in educational experience and possessed of a sound judgment, Doctor Skinner has been an important figure in the teaching of school administrators and teachers. He was held in affectionate regard by his friends and acquaintances in Mexico and vicinity, and by school people, and people interested in schools throughout the state. As proof of the high esteem rendered him by his colleagues, Doctor Skinner was elected honorary president of the Associated Academic Principals, the organization of secondary school principals of the state in 1908. Only one other person, Dr. Charles F. [Wheelock, former assistant commissioner for secondary education, has been so honored.]

[…of America," "History and Government of New York State," and "Community Life in New York." In addition to acting as associate editor of the High School Quarterly, he contributed extensively to educational magazines.]

He was a member of Phi Beta Kappa, and acted as president of the eastern division of the Phi Beta Kappa during the last year. Delta Upsilon, Kappa Phi Kappa, and the Mexico Masonic Order fraternities.

**Survivors** include his wife and two daughters, Mrs. H. O. Fullerton of Albany, and Mrs. Williard G. Taylor of Pulaski. Also seven grandchildren, John, Nancy, Joan, Mary Margaret and Williard Taylor and Avery and James Fullerton.

### Tribute from Mrs. Mary Holmes Richards, Phoenix, N.Y.

"We count our deeply revered and dearly loved friend Dr. Skinner among those 'who gave himself with his alma.' Often in our experience with young people we have gone to him with our problems, and never has he refused his personal interest and valued advice.

"Only a year ago, one of our young friends seemed to be facing unsurmountable discouragements. We advised him to see Dr. Skinner and went with him to the **brick house on Railroad Street**. At his desk, busy with his own affairs, this loyal friend talked with the young man, a complete stranger, as only he could talk, and advised him with wisdom and sympathetic understanding as only he could counsel. As we were leaving we remarked that as long as we three should live, we should be coming to him in the interests of either our own children or those of others; to which he replied with his customary graciousness.

"'All honor him' who stood in high places and stooped to lift up the young of his generation."

Dr. Charles W. Richards
Mrs. Mary Holmes Richards

### Resolutions (School Board of Mexico Academy and Central School)

WHEREAS, God in his infinite wisdom has removed from our midst of our friend and advisor, Avery W. Skinner,

BE IT RESOLVED that we, the members of the School Board of Mexico Academy and Central School, have suffered a great loss and sincerely deplore the fact that he could not have lived to see the completion of this school in which he took such a keen interest. He labored untiringly in its organization and watched with intense interest and pleasure the construction of the building.

We deeply regret that the plans of the Board to have Doctor Skinner occupy a prominent place in the dedication of the structure, cannot be consummated.

BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED, that a copy of this resolution be spread upon the minutes of the School Board, a copy handed to the "Mexico Independent," and a copy presented to members of the bereaved family who have our deep and abiding sympathy in this, their irreparable loss.

Signed: R. Austin Backus, L.D. Pulsifer, Cameron Loomis, Hildred Trim, Leon Valley
