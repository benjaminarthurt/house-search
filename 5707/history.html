History of the House

From a landmark Italianate residence to an American Legion post to our home — 160 years on Scenic Avenue.

National Register of Historic Places

The Timothy Skinner House is listed on the National Register of Historic Places under reference number 91000526, with a listing date of June 20, 1991. The nomination describes it as a large brick residence featuring a 2½-story main block and a 2-story recessed wing, built in the Italianate style circa 1865–1869.

Italianate Architecture

The house is a fine regional example of the Italianate style that swept American residential architecture in the mid-nineteenth century. Characteristic features of the style include:

  • Tall, vertically proportioned massing
  • Low-pitched or visually subdued rooflines
  • Prominent cornices and bracketed eaves
  • Tall, narrow windows with elaborate hood moldings
  • Formal, balanced front compositions
  • Covered porches and decorative exterior detailing

The brick construction and 2½-story main block set the house apart as a substantial residence. The nomination notes that the interior remained substantially intact, with floor-plan changes attributable to the later American Legion occupancy rather than any loss of core historic fabric.

A Timeline

  • c. 1865–1869 Construction. Timothy Warner Skinner, attorney and later Oswego County Surrogate, builds the house on what was then known as Railroad Street (later Center Street, then Scenic Avenue) in the village of Mexico, New York.
  • Late 19th century Skinner family residence. Timothy W. Skinner, his wife Sarah L. Rose Skinner, and their children — including Dr. Avery Warner Skinner, born in the house in 1870 — occupy the home during its most prominent residential era. Timothy serves as village president, law partner of Judge Cyrus Whitney, and prominent Mason.
  • 1915 Timothy W. Skinner dies, March 30, in Mexico. He is buried at Mexico Village Cemetery. His son Avery W. Skinner is cited in his obituary as surviving, then serving in the Albany Education Department.
  • 1922 Stone-Davis Post No. 384, American Legion is documented as active in Mexico, NY, handling local veterans’ services (per a Sandy Creek News notice of May 11, 1922).
  • 1937 Dr. Avery Warner Skinner dies, December 13, in Albany. He is returned home for burial at Mexico Village Cemetery. The last major Skinner family connection to the house era closes.
  • 1944 American Legion acquires the property. The 1961 Mexico Independent Centennial Issue records that in 1944, the Legion purchased “the former home of A. W. Skinner on Scenic Avenue,” converting it into their post and community meeting hall.
  • 1991 Listed on the National Register of Historic Places (NRHP #91000526) as the Timothy Skinner House, June 20, 1991. The nomination describes the Italianate brick residence and notes the American Legion’s floor-plan changes while affirming substantial interior integrity.
  • 2006 American Legion sells the property, returning it to private residential ownership. The county chain for the property records this transfer and the subsequent private ownership period.
  • 2026 Our family acquires the house and begins the next chapter in the life of this 160-year-old landmark.

The American Legion Era

One of the most vivid documentary links between the Skinner family and the modern property is a passage from the 1961 Mexico Independent Centennial Issue, which states that in 1944 the American Legion purchased “the former home of A. W. Skinner on Scenic Avenue.”

“the former home of A. W. Skinner on Scenic Avenue” — Mexico Independent, Centennial Issue, 1961

The NRHP nomination independently records that the house later served as a meeting hall and that the American Legion made some floor-plan changes, while the interior remained substantially intact. The county ownership chain for the property confirms American Legion ownership before the 2006 sale.

The local unit was Stone-Davis Post No. 384. A public memorial page quoting the Sandy Creek News (May 11, 1922) documents the post actively serving the Mexico community — naming it as the organizer of funeral committal services for Pvt. Lyman C. Stacy. A later Syracuse.com reference places Post #384 still active in Mexico as recently as 2013.

The Street That Changed Names

The street on which the house stands has carried three names over its history, which accounts for some variation in how historic records refer to the address:

  • Railroad Street — the earliest recorded name, reflecting the era when the railroad shaped the village
  • Center Street — an intermediate name used in the mid-to-late 19th century
  • Scenic Avenue — the current name, adopted in the 20th century; cited in an 1942 Mexico Independent article on the street’s history

Dr. Avery Warner Skinner’s Find a Grave memorial says he was born in “the historic brick house on Railroad Street” — referring to the same structure now addressed on Scenic Avenue. This three-name chain is one of the key bridges connecting the NRHP nomination records to the current property.
