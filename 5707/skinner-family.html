Genealogy & Legacy

The Skinner Family

For generations, the Skinner name stood at the center of Oswego County public life — as lawyers, judges, educators, legislators, and community builders.

The Family and the House

The house on Scenic Avenue was built by Timothy Warner Skinner, son of the Hon. Avery Skinner, one of the founding civic figures of Mexico, New York. The Skinner family occupied the house through the late nineteenth and into the early twentieth century. The nomination names Timothy W. Skinner as builder and original owner.

The strongest personal link comes from Dr. Avery Warner Skinner, Timothy’s son, whose Find a Grave memorial records that he was “born in the historic brick house on Railroad Street” — Railroad Street being the earlier name for Scenic Avenue. When he died in Albany in 1937, he was brought home to Mexico for burial at the village cemetery.

A 1961 centennial newspaper article in the Mexico Independent records that the American Legion purchased “the former home of A. W. Skinner on Scenic Avenue” in 1944 — making A. W. Skinner (Dr. Avery Warner Skinner) the last named Skinner occupant before the building became a public institution.

Timothy Warner Skinner (1827–1915)

Builder & Original Owner
Timothy Warner Skinner
April 24, 1827 — March 30, 1915 · Mexico, Oswego County, New York

Attorney; law partner of Judge Cyrus Whitney; Oswego County Surrogate; Village President of Mexico; trustee of Mexico Academy; prominent Mason and Methodist. His obituary in the Find a Grave memorial identifies his surviving son Avery W. Skinner of the Albany Education Department and daughter Mrs. J. B. Stone of Auburn. Buried at Mexico Village Cemetery.

Timothy was born in Mexico in 1827 — son of the Hon. Avery Skinner and Elizabeth “Eliza” Huntington. He was admitted to practice law in 1857 and spent his career in Mexico village, rising to serve as Oswego County Surrogate. He built the Italianate house on Railroad Street circa 1865, where he raised his family. He died in Mexico in 1915 at the age of 87.

Dr. Avery Warner Skinner (1870–1937)

Son & Last Named Occupant
Dr. Avery Warner Skinner
August 18, 1870 — December 13, 1937 · Born in Mexico; died in Albany

Educator and New York State official. His Find a Grave memorial explicitly states he was born in the historic brick house on Railroad Street in Mexico. He graduated from Mexico Academy (1887) and Syracuse University (1892). After serving as a school principal in Mexico and Andes, he became Superintendent of Schools in Oneida, then rose to Inspector of History and later Director of Examinations & Inspections for the New York State Education Department. He was returned to Mexico for burial at Mexico Village Cemetery.

Hon. Avery Skinner (1796–1876)

Patriarch of the Oswego County Line
Hon. Avery Skinner
June 9, 1796 (Westmoreland, NH) — November 24, 1876 (Maple View, Oswego County, NY)

Son of Timothy Skinner and Ruth Warner. Came to New York via Watertown; settled at Union Square (later Maple View) in the Town of Mexico. Built and kept a prominent inn; helped establish the post office and mail routes. Served as Oswego County Treasurer, Associate Judge, Assemblyman, State Senator, and long-time Postmaster. Father of a prominent Skinner line, including Timothy Warner Skinner.

Avery Skinner’s career placed the family squarely in the civic history of Oswego County decades before the house was built. His son Timothy would later construct the landmark Italianate residence in Mexico village.

The Broader Skinner Network

The House Line

Timothy Warner Skinner’s Children

Timothy W. Skinner had five children, all born in Mexico. Three died young:

  • Elizabeth Viola “Lizzie” Skinner Stone (1857–1943) — married, later in Auburn, NY; buried at Mexico Village Cemetery
  • Hattie Rose Skinner (January 7, 1860 — May 4, 1861) — died in infancy
  • Mary Eliza Skinner (June 11, 1863 — May 9, 1864) — died in infancy
  • Anna Grace Skinner (June 14, 1868 — December 23, 1894) — died at age 26
  • Dr. Avery Warner Skinner (August 18, 1870 — December 13, 1937) — the longest-lived and most publicly prominent

Notable Collateral Relatives

  • Charles Rufus Skinner (1844–1928) — half-brother of Timothy W.; former Superintendent of Public Instruction for New York State. Timothy’s obituary identifies him as a surviving brother.
  • Rev. James Avery Skinner (1835–1917) — half-brother; minister; died in Manhattan

The breadth and prominence of the Skinner family — lawyers, judges, educators, legislators, and ministers across multiple generations — helps explain both the scale of the house Timothy built and its significance as a local landmark worthy of the National Register.
