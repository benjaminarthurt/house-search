What stands out

1) The 2024 purchase is very recent

Alessio bought in Sept 2024 and the property is now back on market quickly. That is the cleanest practical red flag in the file.

2) The 2020 sale is marked non-arms-length

That does not automatically mean fraud or disaster. It does mean the price is less reliable as a pure market signal. It may reflect:

  • family / related-party transaction
  • unusual deal structure
  • distress or convenience sale
  • investor/off-market arrangement

3) Briggs period is the biggest appreciation block before Alessio

2020 to 2024 goes from $170,200 to $255,000.

That suggests one or more of:

  • property improvements
  • strong local appreciation for this asset type
  • the 2020 price being artificially soft because it was non-arms-length

Likely interpretations

American Legion ownership

American Legion ownership fits the historic-property thread and may tie into the Skinner House story. It does not by itself say anything negative.

Mosqueda period

Looks like a longer-hold owner period with at least one internal title event in 2013.

Sathler period

Short hold. The sale to Briggs being non-arms-length is notable and worth clarifying only if deed or transfer context is easy to obtain.

Briggs period

This is the most likely period when cosmetic or practical value was added, if improvements were made.

Building permit review matters here.

Alessio period

This is the most important ownership question now.

The quick resale could indicate:

  • flip intent
  • discovered issues
  • personal financing / life change
  • strategy shift
  • simply trying to re-list after minor work

Public-record observations about occupancy / use

  • County class remains 2-family residential.
  • County record lists 2 kitchens, which supports either:
  • a true two-unit arrangement,
  • a former two-family setup now used as one house,
  • or a main-house + accessory apartment style layout.
  • A recovered older sold listing page for MLS S1242723 explicitly marketed the house as a two family house and stated the owners lived in the lower apartment while using rent from the other unit to help pay the mortgage.
  • A later 2024 sold-listing text trace says the owners had opened it up as a single family home but that it could be turned back into a 2-unit.
  • At this point, the best evidence says: formerly and functionally 2-family, later reconfigured/marketed as single-family.

Public-source quality on recent owners

Brave web search is producing very little trustworthy, high-signal public material on the recent private owners themselves outside aggregator sites. That means:

  • deed / permit / assessor / MLS history will be much more useful than random people-search results
  • the strongest way to understand owner intent is still through record pattern + listing/permit behavior

Best next evidence to gather

  • building permits from town
  • listing history from 2024 purchase cycle
  • deed packet / transfer details for 2024
  • whether Alessio appears in any legitimate public property or business records beyond this parcel
  • any older listing remarks that describe the house as duplex / apartment / in-law / rental

Bottom line

The ownership pattern is interesting but not automatically alarming until paired with condition evidence. The main concern is not the older chain; it is the short Alessio hold combined with an old house and unclear recent work history. The occupancy/use question also remains open: the file data still leans toward 2-family by record, single-family by current presentation.
